# The Wrong Answer to the Right Questions
## Part One: The Epistemic Collapse

*A series on the real failures that built MAGA — and why MAGA makes every one of them worse*

---

*The public didn't stop trusting institutions because of a demagogue. They stopped because institutions spent the better part of four decades earning that distrust; through definitive claims that proved false, quiet reversals without accountability, and a consistent pattern of treating anyone who noticed as a conspiracy theorist.*

---
![My Father's Shelf showing two fallen soliders flags and mug reading "I love my country it's the government I am afraid of](\assets\images\I_Love_My_Country.jpg) 
On February 29, 2020, Surgeon General Jerome Adams tweeted: "Seriously people — STOP BUYING MASKS!" Eight days later, Anthony Fauci told 60 Minutes that Americans "should not be walking around with masks." Five weeks after that, the CDC reversed course and recommended cloth face coverings for all Americans.

That alone would have been manageable. Science updates its conclusions. That's supposed to be a feature, not a bug. But the reversal came with no honest reckoning. No one said: "We discouraged mask use in part to preserve supply for healthcare workers, and we should have been transparent about that tradeoff." Instead the messaging simply shifted, as if the previous position had never existed, and anyone who remembered being told not to buy masks was treated as though they were imagining things.

The mask reversal was the hairline fracture. The lab leak suppression exposed the fault line beneath it.

On February 19, 2020, twenty-seven scientists published a letter in *The Lancet* condemning "conspiracy theories suggesting that COVID-19 does not have a natural origin." The letter was organized by Peter Daszak, president of EcoHealth Alliance, an organization that had been funneling NIH grant money to the Wuhan Institute of Virology for coronavirus research. He did not disclose this. The letter did not present evidence. It presented a consensus that appeared organic, despite being organized by a figure with a direct institutional stake in the question.

Behind the scenes, the picture was different. On January 31, Scripps researcher Kristian Andersen had emailed Fauci that some features of the virus "(potentially) look engineered." A conference call the next day included Fauci, NIH Director Francis Collins, and other senior scientists. Within weeks, Andersen co-authored a paper in *Nature Medicine* concluding the virus was not a laboratory construct. He later acknowledged the authors' work had been "focused on trying to disprove any type of lab theory." Facebook began removing posts suggesting COVID was man-made. Nobody debated the hypothesis and found it wanting. They labeled it dangerous misinformation and drove it from public discourse, while some of the loudest voices doing so had institutional ties to the research in question.

By 2023, the FBI assessed a lab leak as "most likely" with moderate confidence. The Department of Energy reached the same conclusion at low confidence. Facebook reversed its ban. Three of the original *Lancet* signatories publicly said a lab accident was plausible. The hypothesis went from bannable misinformation to mainstream without anyone responsible for the suppression acknowledging what they'd done.

Then came the schools.

Emails obtained through FOIA show the CDC coordinated its February 2021 school reopening guidance directly with the American Federation of Teachers. AFT Senior Director Kelly Trautner thanked the CDC for "your openness to the suggestions made by our president, Randi Weingarten," and referred to the agency as a "thought partner." Specific AFT-proposed language appeared in the final guidance nearly verbatim. Both AFT and NEA received copies before it was released to the public. CDC Director Walensky had publicly stated the guidance was "free from political meddling."

The AFT had donated nearly $20 million to Democrats in the 2020 cycle. The agency tasked with protecting children's health was incorporating language proposed by a politically aligned organization with a clear stake in the outcome. The cost was not abstract. The National Assessment of Educational Progress documented the largest score declines in reading and mathematics in the history of the assessment, declines concentrated among low-income Black and Hispanic students. The kids who could least afford to lose a year of school lost the most. The agency that was supposed to be making decisions based on science was running its guidance through a union's editorial process.

Would you accept ExxonMobil drafting EPA emissions limits and calling the result independent science?

Mask reversal. Lab leak suppression. School closure capture. Each one alone would dent public trust. Together they revealed something worse than incompetence: a dynamic in which public health authorities treated institutional credibility as a resource to be spent on preferred narratives rather than protected through transparency.

But the COVID failures didn't emerge from nowhere. The soil had been prepared for decades and...

## The Soil Was Watered with Blood

If you want to understand why millions of Americans were primed to distrust their own government long before anyone had heard of COVID-19, start in the mountains of northern Idaho in August 1992.

Randy Weaver was a former Green Beret living off the grid with his family. An ATF informant pressured him into sawing off two shotguns, then the government used the resulting firearms charge as leverage to recruit him as an informant. Weaver refused and missed a court date, partly because the court had sent him a letter with the **wrong date**. The U.S. Marshals Service responded by mounting a months-long surveillance operation involving satellite imagery and a team of camouflaged agents on his rural property. That operation ended on August 21 when marshals encountered Weaver's fourteen-year-old son Sammy and family friend Kevin Harris in the woods. In the ensuing firefight, Deputy U.S. Marshal William Degan was killed. Sammy Weaver was shot in the back while running home. He was fourteen.

The next day, FBI sniper Lon Horiuchi shot Vicki Weaver through the head while she stood in the cabin doorway holding her ten-month-old baby. The shot was taken under rules of engagement that the subsequent Senate investigation found effectively authorized shoot-on-sight, rules so permissive they would have been illegal on any battlefield. Horiuchi said he was aiming at someone else.

The government settled with the Weaver family for $3.1 million. Horiuchi was charged with involuntary manslaughter by the state of Idaho. The charges were dismissed on federal supremacy grounds. No federal agent was criminally prosecuted.

Thirteen months later, the same playbook produced a worse outcome. David Koresh was the leader of the Branch Davidians, a religious sect living in a compound outside Waco, Texas. The ATF wanted him on firearms charges. They could have arrested him in town; he left the compound regularly. Instead they mounted a paramilitary raid on a building full of families. Four agents and six Davidians died in the initial assault. After a 51-day standoff, the FBI pumped CS gas into the compound with tanks, a flammable chemical agent, banned from the battlefield, that is acutely dangerous to children in enclosed spaces. A fire broke out. Seventy-six people burned alive, including 25 children. The cause of the fire remains disputed. The decision to fill a building full of kids with flammable chemical agents does not.

In both cases, the pattern was the same: law enforcement chose the most aggressive possible response to situations that did not require it, escalated when de-escalation was available, and when people died, the system closed ranks. Nobody went to prison. Nobody lost a career. The institutional lesson was clear: there are no consequences for getting it catastrophically wrong, as long as you were just following orders when you did it.

The failures weren't confined to law enforcement. In a different domain, less visible but ultimately more lethal, the same absence of accountability appeared in regulatory decisions. The FDA had approved OxyContin in 1995 on evidence so thin that the reviewing officer left the agency within two years to take a job at Purdue Pharma. More than 800,000 Americans are dead from opioid overdoses since. Nobody went to prison for that either.

I raise Ruby Ridge not to lionize Randy Weaver, who held white separatist views that deserve no defense. I raise it because the facts of the case are not in dispute. The government entrapped a man on a minor charge, surveilled his family for months, killed his son, killed his wife while she held a baby, and then the system ensured that nobody was held accountable. If you are a person living in rural America, watching the governing class explain to you why this was complicated and both sides made mistakes. You remember. And when someone comes along a generation later and tells you the system is rigged and the elites don't care about people like you, your response is not "that's a dangerous demagogue." Your response is "where have you been?"

Ruby Ridge and Waco became founding mythology for the anti-government right. Timothy McVeigh cited both. The militia movement exploded in their aftermath. Some of the people who drew the right conclusions from Ruby Ridge drew catastrophically wrong ones about everything else. McVeigh built a bomb. The movement curdled into paranoia and racism. But a wrong answer to a real problem doesn't make the problem imaginary; it makes the problem more dangerous, because now anyone who raises it gets associated with the worst people who raised it first. The mainstream response was to treat everyone disturbed by these events as a potential domestic terrorist, which only confirmed the suspicion that had created the movement in the first place: the institutions will close ranks, protect their own, and pathologize anyone who objects.

## The Big Lie Before the Big Lie

Then came Iraq.

On September 8, 2002, the *New York Times* published a front-page story by Judith Miller and Michael Gordon reporting that Iraq had sought aluminum tubes for nuclear enrichment. That same morning, Condoleezza Rice, Colin Powell, and Donald Rumsfeld appeared on the Sunday shows citing "a story in the New York Times this morning." The story's sources were inside the administration. The administration cited the story as independent confirmation. The most influential newspaper in the country had functioned, at times, less as a check on power than as a conduit for it, and it was about to help build the case for a war that would cost over 4,400 American lives and, depending on whose count you trust, somewhere between 150,000 and half a million Iraqi civilians. The exact number was never established. Nobody in a position of authority tried hard enough to find out.

The *Times* eventually published an editors' note on May 26, 2004. It ran on page A10. It acknowledged that articles "based on dire claims about Iraq tended to get prominent display, while follow-up articles that called the original ones into question were sometimes buried." The note refused to name reporters. The *Washington Post* fared no better; Bob Woodward later admitted the paper hadn't pushed hard enough, and editor Len Downie acknowledged skeptical reporting was buried while administration claims got front-page treatment.

When no weapons of mass destruction were found, the story shifted to intelligence failures. But it wasn't just an intelligence failure. It was a media failure, an editorial failure, and a failure of every institution that was supposed to serve as a check on executive power in a democracy. The press had one job — ask hard questions before the country went to war — and the prestige outlets abdicated it. A generation of Americans, particularly veterans and military families, learned that the institutions telling them who to trust had gotten the biggest call of their lifetimes catastrophically wrong.

And then nobody lost their job over it.

## The Loop Closes

If Iraq showed the press amplifying the intelligence community's claims without scrutiny, and the COVID era showed public health agencies prioritizing narrative over transparency, the Hunter Biden laptop story showed all of them working in concert.

On October 14, 2020, three weeks before a presidential election, the *New York Post* published emails from a laptop Hunter Biden had abandoned at a Delaware repair shop. Twitter blocked all sharing of the story, including via direct messages. Facebook suppressed its distribution. The *Post*'s account was locked for sixteen days.

The FBI had possessed the laptop since December 2019 and had not identified it as foreign disinformation. When the Bureau's Foreign Influence Task Force met with Twitter and Facebook on the day the story broke, an FBI analyst began to respond about the laptop's authenticity, and was interrupted by an FBI lawyer who said the Bureau had "no further comment." The Bureau possessed evidence that could have prevented the suppression of a true story. It chose silence...

Five days later, fifty-one former senior intelligence officials, including James Clapper, John Brennan, Leon Panetta, and Michael Hayden, published a letter calling the laptop story a Russian information operation. They offered no evidence. They acknowledged they had no evidence. The letter was carefully worded to suggest a conclusion without technically asserting one, and it worked exactly as designed. At the October 22 presidential debate, Joe Biden cited it directly: "There are 50 former national intelligence folks who said that what he's accusing me of is a Russian plant."

Clapper — the same James Clapper who had told Congress under oath in 2013 that the NSA did "not wittingly" collect data on millions of Americans, a statement later exposed by Edward Snowden as flatly false — was now vouching for the suppression of a story whose core claims proved authentic. He called his testimony about NSA surveillance the **"least untruthful"** answer he could give. He was never prosecuted. He became a CNN analyst. Snowden, who exposed the lie, fled the country and hasn't returned. The intelligence community's track record by this point was not a matter of opinion: the DOJ Inspector General had already found 17 significant errors and omissions in the FBI's FISA warrant applications to surveil Carter Page during the Trump-Russia investigation; errors that, per the IG, "made it appear the information supporting probable cause was stronger than was actually the case." An FBI lawyer had literally altered a CIA email, changing Page's status from a CIA source to "not a source," and pleaded guilty to making a false statement. A follow-up audit of 29 additional FISA applications found 209 errors, confirming the problem was systemic, not isolated. This was the institutional backdrop against which fifty-one intelligence officials asked the public to take their word about a laptop.

The *New York Times* authenticated the laptop emails on March 16, 2022, seventeen months after the original story. The *Washington Post* followed two weeks later. Mark Zuckerberg wrote to Congress in August 2024 admitting that the story was not Russian disinformation and that Facebook shouldn't have suppressed it. Former CIA Deputy Director Michael Morell testified that a phone call from Biden campaign advisor Antony Blinken was "the impetus" for organizing the letter.

## Both Directions

The prestige press became politically monolithic from the inside out. The Indiana University "American Journalist" surveys, the most comprehensive longitudinal study of U.S. journalists, based on self-identified political affiliation, document a dramatic shift. In 2002, the Democrat-to-Republican ratio among journalists was roughly 2:1. By 2013, it had reached 4:1. By 2022, it had ballooned to 10.6:1. In certain newsrooms, the ratio is functionally infinite; there are no Republicans. This didn't happen because of a conspiracy. It happened because journalism increasingly drew from the same educational pipeline, the same cities, and the same cultural milieu; the monoculture was emergent, not engineered, and all the more powerful for it. When everyone in the room shares the same assumptions, those assumptions become invisible. They stop being politics and start being reality.

You could see it on the screen in the summer of 2020. On August 26, CNN correspondent Omar Jimenez stood in front of burning cars and buildings in Kenosha, Wisconsin, while the network chyron read: "FIERY BUT MOSTLY PEACEFUL PROTESTS AFTER POLICE SHOOTING." MSNBC's Ali Velshi had done the same from Minneapolis, reporting from in front of a burning building, calling the scene "mostly a protest" that was "not, generally speaking, unruly." Property Claim Services estimated insured losses of $1–2 billion across 140+ cities, making the 2020 unrest the costliest civil disorder in U.S. insurance history, surpassing the 1992 LA riots. Between 19 and 25 deaths were associated with the violence. And it wasn't a one-off. The habit of narrative-first, facts-later journalism had already produced the Covington Catholic debacle, where multiple outlets destroyed a teenager's reputation based on a misleadingly clipped video, leading to defamation settlements from CNN, the *Washington Post*, and NBC, and the Jussie Smollett case, where presidential candidates called a fabricated attack a "modern-day lynching" before basic police work revealed it was staged, and the media machine that amplified the hoax never circled back with anything resembling proportional correction. When what the audience could see with their own eyes contradicted what the chyron told them to believe, the credibility cost was visceral. Millions of people watched a building burn while a journalist in front of it told them things were mostly fine, and they drew the obvious conclusion about what else they were being told was mostly fine.

So did the right demand ideological diversity in newsrooms? Push for better journalism? No, they built their own captured media, from the outside in, through acquisition and consolidation.

Sinclair Broadcast Group owns or operates roughly 185 television stations reaching about 40% of American households. In March 2018, dozens of Sinclair anchors across the country read a nearly identical script warning viewers about "the troubling trend of irresponsible, one-sided news stories plaguing our country." The compilation video, local anchors from Boise to Baltimore mouthing the same words in eerie unison, went viral. It should have.

These weren't Fox News hosts or talk radio personalities. They were the people who read you the weather and covered your local city council, now serving as a distribution network for centrally produced editorial content. Sinclair mandated "must-run" segments from Boris Epshteyn, a former Trump White House official, that stations were required to air regardless of local editorial judgment. Call that journalism with a conservative lean if you want. It was an ideological distribution pipeline wearing the skin of your trusted local news anchor.

And then there was Fox News, the most-watched cable news network in America, the gravitational center of the right-wing media ecosystem, and the subject of the **largest media defamation settlement in American history.**

In April 2023, Fox News settled with Dominion Voting Systems for $787.5 million. But the settlement isn't the story. The discovery is. Internal texts and emails, produced under oath and entered into the court record, showed that Fox hosts and executives privately acknowledged the 2020 election fraud claims were false while promoting them on air. Tucker Carlson texted that Sidney Powell was "lying" and her claims were "insane." He continued to platform them. Sean Hannity said he never believed the Dominion narrative "for one second." Rupert Murdoch testified under oath that he wished Fox hosts had been "stronger in denouncing" the stolen election claims. The network aired the claims anyway because the audience demanded them and the ratings required them. When Fox's decision desk correctly called Arizona for Biden on election night, the internal backlash was about business, not truth. The network was hemorrhaging viewers to Newsmax and OANN. Executives feared that reporting reality would cost them market share.

A network whose own hosts called the claims insane, aired them anyway because the truth was bad for business, and then paid three-quarters of a billion dollars to make it go away... Structurally identical to the *Times* running Judith Miller's WMD stories, an institution that knew or should have known better, choosing narrative over truth because the incentive structure rewarded it. The only difference is that Fox left a paper trail and got caught.

The left captured the prestige press through cultural selection. The right captured local news through corporate consolidation and cable through audience capture. Neither side wanted a free press. Both wanted *their* press. And the public, bombarded from both directions by outlets that had substituted advocacy for reporting, did the rational thing: they stopped believing any of it.

---

Here is the throughline: Ruby Ridge to Waco to Iraq to Snowden to COVID to the laptop. Fox News to Sinclair to CNN standing in front of a fire calling it peaceful. Covington to Smollett to 800,000 opioid dead. Different domains, different actors, different decades; the same structure: authority asserted, errors concealed, accountability deferred. More than three decades of it. Each failure eroded trust. Each failure was followed by institutional circling of the wagons. Each time someone noticed, they were called a conspiracy theorist — until the next revelation proved them at least partially right.

People call the cumulative effect cynicism. It looks more like rational pattern recognition.

And this is the abbreviated version. We haven't gotten to the Rolling Stone gang rape story that never happened, which destroyed a fraternity and a university administrator's career before the magazine retracted it and paid $1.65 million in settlements. We haven't gotten to the FBI fabricating evidence in FISA warrant applications as a matter of routine: 209 errors across 29 applications, per the Inspector General's audit. We haven't gotten to the FDA's revolving door sending regulators to the companies they just finished regulating, or NIH scientists collecting $350 million in pharmaceutical royalties revealed only through years of FOIA litigation. We haven't gotten to Fauci's senior advisor describing the use of personal email to evade public records requests: "I can either send stuff to Tony on his private gmail, or hand it to him at work... He is too smart to let colleagues send him stuff that could cause trouble." We haven't gotten to the intelligence community's entire Russia investigation, which the Durham report concluded was launched without "any actual evidence of collusion" and with "a serious lack of analytical rigor." The catalog is not complete. It can't be. How long does a list have to get before the pattern stops being a coincidence?

## The Wrong Answer

In 2016, voting for Trump was a defensible act of protest. The institutions had failed. Both parties had ignored the people most affected. The man said he saw it, and he'd fix it. And between 2016 and 2024, the Democrats did almost nothing to prove him wrong. They presided over the very COVID failures this piece documents. They dismissed legitimate concerns as misinformation. They lost the trust of working-class voters they'd already been losing for a generation and responded by calling those voters bigots. So when 2024 came around and people pulled the lever for Trump again, many of them weren't choosing him so much as refusing to choose the people who'd spent four years confirming everything he said about them. That logic made sense.

The problem is what it bought.

Trump didn't look at institutions that had failed and ask how to reform them. He didn't demand transparency requirements for public health agencies, or structural independence for the CDC, or accountability mechanisms for intelligence officials who lie to Congress. He didn't push for media that was more rigorous, from either side. He didn't rebuild trust. He declared all trust illegitimate, except trust in him.

"Fake news" replaced a broken epistemology with no epistemology at all. The message collapsed from "these institutions need to be more honest" into "nothing is true except what I tell you." The destination was never an informed public capable of holding power accountable; it was an audience.

And when you hand someone that kind of authority, when you tell 74 million people that every institution is lying except this one man, you get what we got this spring.

On March 9, 2026, Donald Trump told the American public that the war with Iran was "very complete, pretty much." He claimed the Iranian military had been destroyed and the Strait of Hormuz had reopened. A month later, standing in the White House briefing room, he told AFP: "Total and complete victory. 100%. No question about it." Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth echoed him from the Pentagon: "Operation Epic Fury was a historic and overwhelming victory on the battlefield. A capital-V military victory."

Here is what was actually true at the moment those words were spoken: thirteen American service members were dead. Hundreds more were wounded. Iran had closed the Strait of Hormuz for the first time in modern history and effectively established itself as gatekeeper of the waterway, a fundamentally different and worse strategic position for the United States than existed before the conflict. Iran's foreign minister was publicly calling Trump's characterizations "false and baseless." At the very press conference where Trump claimed the U.S. had lost no planes to enemy fire, the Joint Chiefs chairman confirmed that an F-15 had been shot down by Iran and an A-10 had been lost to Iranian fire during the rescue mission. Trump contradicted his own Pentagon, in real time, from the same podium.

This is the CDC mask reversal in a flight suit. An authoritative figure making a definitive claim that is contradicted by observable, verifiable reality. The institutional apparatus around him reorganizing to protect the claim rather than correct it. The only difference is the body count. This is the man who altered a NOAA weather map with a Sharpie rather than admit he misspoke about a hurricane's path, and then had the Commerce Department threaten to fire the NOAA chief if the agency didn't back his version. That was absurd. This is the same instinct applied to a war in which Americans came home in coffins and the strait that carries a fifth of the world's oil supply fell under hostile control. The president looked into a camera and said "total and complete victory."

When CNN stood in front of a burning building and called the scene "mostly peaceful," it became the most mocked moment in modern broadcast journalism. The right, correctly, identified it as institutional reality-denial in service of a political narrative. Now consider: the president stood at a podium and declared total victory in a war where the enemy controls the waterway we went to war to reopen, where American planes were shot from the sky, where thirteen families are planning funerals. If the CNN chyron was gaslighting, what is this?

The same dynamic extends to the policy framework of his entire second term. "China pays the tariffs" is a claim Trump has repeated hundreds of times across both terms. Not a matter of ideological disagreement; not a question of partisan framing. It is flatly, demonstrably false by the unanimous consensus of economists across the political spectrum, including his own advisors. Tariffs are paid by American importers and passed to American consumers as higher prices. This is Econ 101. It is as settled as the relationship between smoking and cancer. And the entire institutional right has reorganized itself around not challenging it, exactly the way the institutional left reorganized around not challenging COVID orthodoxies. The machinery is identical. Only the decals have changed.

He didn't drain the swamp of institutional dishonesty. He franchised it.

When he returned to power, he had the opportunity to do what the moment demanded: reform the agencies that had failed, install leaders committed to transparency, and rebuild the credibility that decades of institutional arrogance had squandered. Instead he installed Robert F. Kennedy Jr. at HHS, a man who replaced the CDC's brand of narrative management with his own brand of conspiratorial thinking. He didn't fix the FDA's revolving door with the pharmaceutical industry. He didn't impose conflict-of-interest rules on intelligence officials. He didn't protect whistleblowers or strengthen FOIA. When career prosecutors refused to participate in the lying, they were forced out. The office created after Watergate to prosecute government corruption was reduced from 36 lawyers to two. (For the full accounting of what followed, see [Provable Lies: What They Said vs. What Actually Happened](https://medium.com/@cwood92/provable-lies-5377f8e4fb99).)

He built a parallel media ecosystem, from OANN to Telegram channels to Sinclair's must-run segments, more captured, less accurate, and entirely unaccountable, but loyal to him. Fox News paid three-quarters of a billion dollars for knowingly broadcasting lies about an election. The outlets Trump elevated don't even have the infrastructure to be sued; they operate below the waterline of accountability entirely.

The institutions failed. That is documented, unambiguous, and undeniable. But a wrecking ball is not a renovation crew. And a man who looks into a camera and says "total and complete victory" while American planes are falling out of the sky and the strait is under enemy control is doing exactly what the institutions did. He is telling you what he needs you to believe.

The people who lost trust in these institutions were right to lose it. The question was never whether the anger was justified. It was always what you do with it.

And "hand it to a man who will use it to lie to your face about a war" was never the answer.
